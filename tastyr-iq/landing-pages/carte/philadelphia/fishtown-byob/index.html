The Fishtown BYOB Circuit

Fishtown, Philadelphia
Frankford Ave + cross streets
April 2026
ForkFox Tested
31
BYOB restaurants within a one-mile radius of Frankford Ave — a density that exists nowhere else in the country and is entirely the fault of Pennsylvania's liquor laws.

Pennsylvania's state liquor control system is, objectively, one of the stranger institutions in American civic life. You cannot buy wine at a grocery store. Beer requires a trip to a separate license class of establishment. The PLCB has a monopoly so complete that it borders on parody. But somewhere in that tangle of Prohibition-era legislation, a loophole emerged: restaurants that don't hold a liquor license can let you bring your own. And in Fishtown, where the rent is still (relatively) manageable and the chefs are ambitious, that loophole became a culture.

Essential Stops on the Circuit
01
Loco Pez
2401 Frankford Ave · No reservations
Mexican BYOB that punches well above its price point. The al pastor is carved from a trompo and not a freezer bag, which matters more than it sounds. Bring a six-pack of Modelo, show up at 6pm before the line forms, and order the fish tacos and the elotes. This is the kind of neighborhood place that quietly becomes the answer to "where do you always take people."
No Reservations
02
Wm. Mulherin's Sons
1355 N Front St · Reservations recommended
Wood-fired pizza inside a restored 19th-century whiskey blending facility. The building alone is worth the trip — exposed brick, high ceilings, a bar that runs the length of the room — but the food follows through. The cacio e pepe pizza is better than it has any right to be. Bring a bottle of natural wine. They'll give you a nice glass for it.
Best Space
03
Pizzeria Beddia
1313 N Lee St · Very limited seating
Once voted the best pizza in America, now slightly easier to get into since moving to a larger space. Still outstanding. The approach is ingredient-first: good cheese, good tomato, good dough, don't mess it up. It's a philosophy that works. Cash and carry was the original model; the new location has seats and a slightly longer menu. Go on a Wednesday.
Legendary

The economic logic of Fishtown BYOB is worth understanding because it explains why the food is so good. A restaurant with a liquor license typically makes 70–80% of its profit on drinks. Without that safety net, the kitchen has to carry the whole operation. There's no $14 cocktail to subsidize a mediocre entrée. The food is the product, which means the chefs who choose this model tend to be the ones who actually believe in their food. The places that survive more than two years on Frankford Ave are the ones where someone in the kitchen is working harder than they need to.

The unwritten rule: stop at the wine shop two doors down from wherever you're eating. Most Fishtown BYOBs are surrounded by bottle shops that know exactly what their neighbors serve.

The math always works out in your favor. A $12 bottle from the shop around the corner, a $38 dinner for two, no reservation required at most spots. You'll spend $60 on a meal that would cost $110 with a poured wine list attached. Pennsylvania's liquor laws are absurd and probably shouldn't exist. The restaurants they accidentally created are some of the best in the country.
