Ethiopian Food on Baltimore Ave

West Philadelphia
Baltimore Ave 42nd–50th
April 2026
ForkFox Tested
23
dishes tested across 8 spots on a single stretch of Baltimore Ave — one of the most underrated East African food corridors in the northeast.

Baltimore Ave between 42nd and 50th doesn't look like a food destination. It's a residential stretch of West Philadelphia — tree-lined, low-key, a few corner stores and hair salons mixed in. Most visitors pass through on the way somewhere else. But get out of the car and you'll find something that mainstream food media keeps missing: a dense, decades-old pocket of East African restaurants that are not Ethiopian-inspired, not Ethiopian-adjacent, but the real thing — run by immigrants from Addis Ababa and Dire Dawa who have been feeding this neighborhood since before it was on anyone's radar.

Top Picks on This Corridor
01
Dahlak Restaurant
4708 Baltimore Ave · Cash only
The anchor of the corridor since 1993. Walk in expecting nothing fancy and leave unable to stop thinking about the kitfo — beef tartare warmed in spiced clarified butter, served alongside ayib (house-made fresh cheese) and torn injera. Order the full platter if you have four people. You'll need them.
Since '93
02
Abyssinia Restaurant
229 S 45th St · Around the corner
Quieter, better for two people, and the place to go if you want the best doro wat in Philadelphia. The slow-braised chicken stew — built on a base of berbere and niter kibbeh, with a hard-boiled egg simmered into the sauce — takes hours to make properly. It tastes like those hours.
Best Doro Wat
03
Aksum Bar & Grill
4630 Baltimore Ave
Go vegetarian here even if you're not. The beyaynetu — the full vegetarian spread on injera — is a masterclass in how Ethiopian cuisine handles legumes and greens. The alicha, a mild yellow split-pea stew spiced with turmeric rather than berbere, is somehow more interesting than the heat-forward options. Order the yekik alicha. You'll be back for it.
Best Veg

What distinguishes this corridor from the Ethiopian restaurants scattered across other American cities is how uncompromising the food is. The injera is fermented the right amount — sour, spongy, slightly elastic — not the pale commercial approximation that shows up where the kitchen is hedging for outside tastes. The berbere spice blends are house-made. The honey wine, tej, when it's on the menu, is actually tej. This food is cooked for the West Philly community that's been here for thirty-plus years, and that community has opinions. Baltimore Ave's Ethiopian population arrived in waves through the '80s and '90s, and the places that survived did so by cooking for regulars who know exactly what it's supposed to taste like.

A full platter at Dahlak includes kitfo, tibs, misir wot, and ayib — served communally on a single sheet of injera. There are no plates. That is the point.

ForkFox tested 23 dishes across eight spots on this stretch through early 2026. The neighborhood eats as well as it ever has — possibly better. Not one of these restaurants appears on the first page of a Google search for "best Ethiopian food in Philadelphia." Which is, if you know where to look, the best possible sign.
