The Mission Burrito War

Mission District, San Francisco
16th to 24th St
April 2026
Deep Dive
47
burritos eaten in the Mission District in a single month. One format, three camps, zero consensus — and the debate has been running since the Carter administration.

There is no neutral ground in the Mission burrito debate. You're La Taqueria or you're El Farolito, and if you don't have a strong opinion, you haven't been paying attention. This argument has consumed food writers, divided families, and generated enough takes on the internet to fill a small library. We ate 47 burritos over four weeks across 11 taquerias on the Mission 16th-to-24th corridor to either settle it or at least understand it better. We did not settle it. We understand it completely.

The Three Main Camps
01
La Taqueria
2889 Mission St · Open until 9pm
No rice. This is the thing people argue about. Miguel Jara Sr. decided in the 1970s that rice diluted the burrito and he was right. The carne asada is marinated, grilled to order, and not overwhelmed by anything else. The tortilla is steamed then briefly griddled on a dry comal. The result is denser, more concentrated, and more expensive than most competitors. The people who prefer it will not hear any argument.
No Rice Camp
02
El Farolito
2779 Mission St · Open until 3:30am weekends
The counterargument. Rice goes in. The tortilla is larger. The portions are more generous. Open until 3:30am on weekends, which means this burrito has absorbed a lot of complicated feelings over the years. The super burrito here — with sour cream, guacamole, cheese, rice, beans, and your choice of meat — is one of the most satisfying $12 objects in the Bay Area. It is also, and this matters, available when nothing else is.
The Night Shift
03
Taqueria Cancun
2288 Mission St · Two locations
The compromise candidate that everyone can agree on, which makes it the least exciting pick but the most consistently correct one. The al pastor is carved properly, the rice and beans are well-seasoned, and the line moves fast. If you're bringing someone to the Mission for the first time and you don't want to explain the theology of the La Taqueria rice debate, this is where you go. The burrito will be excellent. No explanation required.
Safe Consensus

The Mission-style burrito — large steamed flour tortilla, filled and wrapped tight — was effectively born here in the 1960s, with El Faro generally credited as the originator of the format, though the thing that most people think of as a Mission burrito (the super, the overstuffed foil-wrapped log) got fully developed in the 1980s. The neighborhood was then largely working-class Mexican and Central American, the taquerias opened to feed the community, and the food was priced accordingly. A lot has changed about the Mission since then. The burritos have not changed much, which is the most important thing about them.

La Taqueria's carne asada burrito, cross-sectioned. No rice. The density-to-surface-area ratio is different from a rice-inclusive burrito. This is why people have strong opinions.

The right answer is to eat at all three. The debate keeps the standards high and the portions honest. In a city where a $22 avocado toast gets a TechCrunch profile, La Taqueria is still selling roughly the same burrito it has for decades, at a price that has barely moved. That's not nostalgia. That's a standard being held.
