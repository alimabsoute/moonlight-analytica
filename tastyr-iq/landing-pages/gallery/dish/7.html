The Har Gow Test

Har gow — steamed shrimp dumplings — is the single most reliable quality indicator in dim sum. A good wrapper is thin enough to be translucent, flexible enough to pick up without tearing, and thick enough to hold its pleating without collapsing. The shrimp filling should snap clean when bitten, not compress into a paste. The dough composition and steaming time are the critical variables, and getting both right simultaneously requires a level of calibration that most kitchens never achieve.

Why Age Matters Here

The kitchen in question has the same dim sum chef it had in 1994. The muscle memory for wrapper thickness, the timing of the bamboo steamer rotation, the filling ratio — these are calibrated to near-perfection through repetition. Modern dim sum operations that opened in the last decade mostly compete on interior design and Instagram-friendly novelty items. This kitchen competes on technical execution, and it wins.
