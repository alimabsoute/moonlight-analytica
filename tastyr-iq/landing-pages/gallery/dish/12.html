Al pastor is a Mexican taco tradition derived from Lebanese shawarma brought to Mexico City by Lebanese immigrants in the early 20th century. The meat — thinly sliced pork marinated in dried chiles, achiote, and pineapple — is stacked vertically on a trompo (vertical spit) and slow-cooked while rotating. The taquero shaves the meat directly onto a tortilla as orders come in, adding a pineapple slice from the top of the spit.

The trompo is not a theatrical prop. It is the technique. Without it, you are making something else and calling it al pastor.

Why Most Al Pastor in the US Gets It Wrong

The majority of al pastor served in American taquerias is prepared in a pan, on a flat-top griddle, or in a conventional oven — not on a vertical spit. This produces meat with an entirely different texture and flavor profile: crispier in some cases, yes, but lacking the layered fat rendering and chile penetration that a properly operated trompo produces over 4-6 hours of rotation. Our model scores trompo vs. non-trompo separately, and the gap is significant.

The Pilsen Standard

The taqueria our algorithm identified in Pilsen operates with a two-trompo setup — one for service, one building the next column. The marinade is achiote-heavy with a dried guajillo and ancho base, pineapple juice used in the marinade rather than only as topping, and a chile de árbol layer for heat that builds slowly rather than hitting immediately. The tortillas are handmade on-site from masa imported from Jalisco twice weekly.

The combination of authentic spit technique, house-made tortillas, and a chile profile that requires genuine knowledge to construct puts this taqueria in a tier by itself in the Midwest. We scored it 91 overall, with a 97 on technique and 94 on flavor profile. It costs $3.50 per taco and is open until 1am Thursday through Saturday.
