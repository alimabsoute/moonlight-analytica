Before Queens Forgot What Birria Tasted Like

A visual document of a Corona, Queens birria kitchen that has resisted every trend pressure and kept making the same 10-hour braise for four years.

ForkFox Algorithm · March 2026 · Score: 91 · 5 min read

The Corona, Queens kitchen — Friday morning, 7am, two hours before service. The consommé has been on the stove since 11pm the night before.

The 10-Hour Braise

The birria at this kitchen is a beef-goat blend — 60% beef short rib and chuck, 40% bone-in goat — braised in a dried chile sauce made from ancho, guajillo, and morita, plus cinnamon, cloves, oregano, and thyme. The cook time is 10 hours minimum. The kitchen doesn't have a timer. The cook checks the meat by feel: when the bones pull free with no resistance, it's done.

The consommé at the 8-hour mark — brick-red, glossy, a thin film of fat on the surface that indicates correct rendering.

The Tortilla Detail

The tortillas here are made from masa that is mixed fresh each morning with warm water, salt, and a small amount of lard. They are pressed to approximately 3mm and griddled on a comal until they take on a light char on both sides — the char is intentional, a textural element that adds bitterness to balance the richness of the birria fat. When dipped in consommé before griddling, the brick-red color saturates the tortilla completely.

The Finished Taco

Consommé-dipped tortilla, griddled until crispy. Braised birria beef-goat blend. Diced white onion, cilantro, fresh salsa verde. Consommé in a small cup alongside. Every component measured and scored independently.
