The Siu Mai That Changed How We Score Cantonese

A Richmond District kitchen produces pork-shrimp siu mai that redefined our model's baseline for wrapper-to-filling ratio and texture calibration.

The Siu Mai Construction Problem

Siu mai is deceptively simple — a thin wheat wrapper folded around a pork and shrimp filling, open-top, steamed. The difficulty lies in wrapper thickness: too thin and the wrapper tears during steaming; too thick and it dominates the filling and turns gummy. The correct thickness, approximately 1.2mm, requires a dough that is worked to exactly the right hydration and rolled immediately before folding. Pre-made wrappers, which most operations use, are made days earlier and have a fundamentally different texture profile.

The Broader Dataset

We scored 31 dim sum operations across the Bay Area. Of those, 11 used fresh-made wrappers. All 11 scored above the dataset mean on wrapper texture. Of the 20 using commercial pre-made wrappers, none scored above 80 on that dimension. The correlation is not a coincidence — it reflects a technique decision that has direct, measurable consequences on the finished product. The Richmond District kitchen's decision to roll fresh every morning is not a marketing choice. It is the foundation of its score.
