Lincolnwood is an inner suburb directly north of Chicago, separated from the city by nothing more than a street sign. It has a significant Japanese-American population, anchored by a Japanese grocery store that has been there since the 1970s, and the food ecosystem around that community has produced several operations that our model has flagged as significantly above the city average.

The Broth at 97

A broth construction score of 97 is the highest our tonkotsu dimension has produced in a non-coastal market. It reflects a product that is milky, viscous, and correctly emulsified from a 14-hour bone extraction process that uses femur bones split in half lengthwise — a technique that exposes more marrow surface area and produces a richer final broth than whole-bone methods.

"Splitting the femur bones exposes more marrow surface area — more contact between the marrow fat and the broth, more extraction depth. It's a 20-minute prep step that is visible in the finished product."

The tare — a combination of shio (salt) and a small amount of white miso — integrates into the broth at service rather than being pre-mixed, which maintains the broth's freshness and allows the seasoning balance to be adjusted per bowl. The noodles are ordered fresh from a Japanese-American noodle maker in Des Plaines. The chashu is braised pork belly, 4 hours, rolled and pressed. This kitchen is operating at a level that the city doesn't know about yet. It won't stay that way.
