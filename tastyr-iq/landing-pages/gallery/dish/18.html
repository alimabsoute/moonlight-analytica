The Miso Ramen Nobody in Midtown Is Talking About

After New York's ramen boom plateau, the scene is sorting itself. One Astoria operation making Northern Japanese Sapporo-style miso ramen is emerging as the city's most technically accomplished bowl.

The Sapporo Style Difference

Sapporo-style miso ramen is a northern Japanese tradition built for cold weather: a rich, warm, fatty broth made from a blend of chicken and pork stock emulsified with a miso tare that has been fermented for a minimum of 12 months. The tare is the flavor center — a complex, deeply savory, slightly sweet paste that defines the bowl's character. The Astoria kitchen uses a house-made hatcho and shiro miso blend that is their most closely guarded recipe.

"The tare is the flavor center. A miso ramen's quality ceiling is set entirely by the quality and fermentation depth of its tare — everything else is execution."

Sapporo-style miso ramen is typically finished with a pat of corn butter — an addition that adds richness and sweetness that balances the fermented depth of the tare.

The Broth

14-hour pork-chicken double stock, emulsified with house miso tare at time of service. Fat content is high and intentional.

The Noodles

Thick, wavy house-made noodles — the correct Sapporo specification — at 42% hydration. They hold up to the heavy broth for the full eating window.

The Toppings

Chashu, corn, bean sprouts, soft egg, and a pat of corn butter. Traditional. Correct. Nothing clever or disruptive.
