Kitfo is Ethiopian beef tartare — raw or lightly warmed minced beef seasoned with mitmita (a dry chile-cardamom spice blend) and niter kibbeh (clarified spiced butter). It is a luxury dish in Ethiopian cuisine, typically eaten on special occasions, and it is almost never made correctly in the diaspora.

The mitmita is the tell. A properly made mitmita contains 11 to 14 individual spices — bird's eye chiles, cardamom, cloves, coriander, rue, korarima, and others — ground fresh and balanced for heat, warmth, and aromatic depth simultaneously. A commercial mitmita substitute, or a simplified version made with fewer ingredients, produces a one-dimensional spice profile that experienced palates will immediately identify as incomplete.

The Beef Specification

The beef in a proper kitfo should be minced by hand from a single fresh cut — typically tenderloin or sirloin — on the day of service, not ground through a mechanical grinder. The texture difference between hand-minced and machine-ground beef is significant: hand-minced retains individual muscle fiber integrity, producing a bite that has texture and cohesion without the paste-like consistency that mechanical grinding creates. The West Philadelphia kitchen serving our top-scored kitfo sources its beef from a local halal butcher and minces it by hand.

The kitfo here is served with ayib (fresh Ethiopian cheese) and gomen (collard greens), which are the traditional accompaniments. The three components together — rich fatty beef, clean fresh cheese, bitter greens — form a nutritional and flavor architecture that has been calibrated over centuries. It is one of the most complete dishes in any cuisine, and this kitchen's version is exceptional. Score: 93.
