Chicago deep dish exists in two parallel universes. In the tourist version — the one with the hour-long lines on Michigan Avenue — the pizza is an engineering project: high-walled, sauce-heavy, aggressively photogenic. In the local version, deep dish is a neighborhood dish that is dense but not ponderous, sauced with restraint, and sized for actual human consumption without theatrical documentation.

Construction Matters More Than Ingredients

The critical variable in deep dish is the crust-to-filling ratio, and most operations get this wrong by overcorrecting toward filling. A proper deep dish crust has a cornmeal composition that creates structural integrity — it should hold its form when sliced without collapsing — and the wall height should cap at approximately 2 inches. Beyond that, you're making a casserole.

"A proper deep dish crust holds its form when sliced without collapsing. Most tourist-facing operations make a casserole and call it pizza."

The Address Our Model Found

The operation our algorithm surfaced is a 40-year-old counter in Bridgeport, one neighborhood removed from the tourist circuit. It uses Italian sausage from a butcher two blocks away, whole-milk mozzarella in a single layer rather than the typical overloaded multiple layers, and a chunky tomato sauce with minimal seasoning that lets the quality of the tomatoes make the argument. The crust is 1.75 inches. Every slice holds. The wait is never more than 15 minutes.
