A Taqueria in the Mission That Still Does Everything Right

Forty years, same address, same carnitas recipe. A visual record of what commitment to a single dish looks like when it matures correctly.

The Mission District taqueria as it appears on a Saturday morning, before the line forms.

Forty Years of Carnitas

The carnitas recipe at this Mission taqueria has not changed since 1985. Pork shoulder braised in lard with orange, milk, Coca-Cola, and bay leaf until the fat renders and the exterior crisps under its own weight. The recipe is written on a card in the owner's handwriting, laminated, and taped inside a cabinet door that no customer ever sees.

The lard braising pot, the finished carnitas, and the hand-pressed tortillas that accompany them.

The Tortilla Operation

The tortillas are pressed from masa prepared each morning from dried maize — not masa harina, which is pre-processed — purchased from a Oaxacan supplier in the East Bay. The tortilla press operator has worked here for 22 years. She produces 800 tortillas before 9am. Each tortilla is griddled to order on a comal that has been seasoned for decades and is, effectively, irreplaceable.

The combination of a proper lard-braised carnitas and a freshly pressed masa tortilla is so far above the city's average taco quality that the comparison is nearly unfair.
