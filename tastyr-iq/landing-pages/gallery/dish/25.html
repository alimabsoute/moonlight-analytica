Little Ethiopia LA:
The Tibs That
Defined the Category

Sautéed beef tibs — cubed, spiced, served on injera — is the most common Ethiopian restaurant order in America. After 28 visits to Little Ethiopia on Fairfax Avenue, our model found the one address that has calibrated it correctly.

Why Tibs Gets Overlooked

Tibs is sautéed beef, typically cubed tenderloin or sirloin, cooked in a pan with berbere, rosemary, jalapeño, and onion. It's ordered more than any other dish at Ethiopian restaurants in the US, which has had the counterintuitive effect of making it less prestigious — it's too common to be taken seriously by food media.

Our model doesn't share this bias. It scores tibs on the same rigorous dimension framework it applies to everything else, and the findings from Little Ethiopia are notable: the gap between the best and worst tibs on Fairfax is 44 points. The best is genuinely exceptional.

The Benchmark

The top-scoring tibs on Fairfax Avenue uses tenderloin cubed to a consistent 3/4-inch size — larger than most operations, which produces a better crust-to-interior ratio. The berbere is house-made from a spice blend that includes rue, a bitter herb rarely used in American-adapted versions of the spice blend. The rosemary is fresh, not dried. The jalapeño is added early to develop sweetness, not late for heat.

"The berbere is house-made with rue — a bitter herb rarely included in Americanized versions. That ingredient alone shifts the flavor profile into a different register."

The tibs arrives sizzling in a pan placed on a thick injera. The injera is 72-hour fermented teff, produced in-house. The sourness is present and clean — not sharp, not muted. The tibs and the injera work as a system: the fat from the meat seasons the injera as you eat, and the sourness of the injera cuts the richness of the meat.
