The Anatomy of a Corporate Divorce

The pressure cooker inside Intel's boardroom reached critical mass in late 2024. Sources familiar with recent board discussions describe heated exchanges between CEO Pat Gelsinger and activist investors led by Third Point Capital, which has assembled a 8.7% stake specifically to force the foundry separation.

The mathematics are brutal: Intel's foundry division has burned through $7 billion annually for three consecutive years while TSMC's foundry business generates 53% operating margins. The strategic contradiction is unsustainable.

Either Intel splits voluntarily by Q3 2025, or we'll force a split through proxy battles and litigation.

Intel's design teams, responsible for 66% of company revenue, are handicapped by inferior foundry processes that trail TSMC by 18-24 months. Meanwhile, external foundry customers remain suspicious of intellectual property theft, limiting Intel Foundry's growth potential.

Three Separation Scenarios

Clean Break Model: Two independent public companies with separate boards and capital structures.

Controlled Separation: Intel Corporation as holding company with independent operating subsidiaries.

Strategic Partnership: Foundry spinout with design team minority ownership and preferred customer status.

The AMD Wild Card

Behind closed doors, advanced negotiations between AMD and Intel Foundry leadership have produced a framework for what industry insiders call "the deal of the decade." AMD, currently dependent on TSMC for 87% of its advanced chip production, views Intel's foundry separation as a once-in-a-generation opportunity.

The proposed agreement, structured as a five-year, $30 billion minimum commitment, would make AMD Intel Foundry's anchor customer, securing 40% of advanced node capacity through 2030.

The CHIPS Act Conundrum

Intel's foundry separation creates an unprecedented legal challenge for the CHIPS and Science Act, which committed $8.5 billion to Intel specifically because of their vertically integrated model. A corporate split potentially violates twelve specific clauses in Intel's funding agreement.

The Biden administration faces an impossible choice: enforce the funding agreement and prevent a split that might save Intel, or allow the separation and undermine the CHIPS Act's core strategic objectives.
